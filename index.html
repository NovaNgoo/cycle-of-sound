01 — Concept

Sound is
never still

This project explores the idea that cycles are embedded in everything — from the rhythm of a heartbeat to the repeating base pairs of DNA, from the pattern of rain on a drum to the seasons cycling through a year.

Rather than treating sound as a product of instruments alone, this installation asks: what if every cycle in nature is already music?

Inspired by soundscape ecologist Bernie Krause's concept of biophony, and the visual works of Hiroshi Sugimoto, the project seeks to make the invisible audible — to give form to repetition itself.

"There's no separation between sound and visuals."
— Ryoji Ikeda
"Natural environments behave like musical compositions, where every species contributes a repeating, cyclical rhythm."
— Bernie Krause, Soundscape Ecologist
02 — The Pulse

Everything begins with a heartbeat

The heart's pumping cycle produces vibrations in patterns that mirror our emotional state — fast with excitement, slow with calm.

Live ECG simulation — the foundation of the installation's audio loop
03 — Artistic Influences

The artists who
shaped this work

Artist 01

Bernie Krause

Soundscape ecologist who spent 50+ years recording natural environments. He coined biophony (collective animal sound), geophony (natural non-animal sound), and anthropophony (human-created sound). His insight: nature itself composes.

Soundscape Ecology
Artist 02

Hiroshi Sugimoto

Japanese photographer who depicted cycles of nature through long exposures — tides, day to night, endless waves. His monochrome images each carry a subtle rhythm; you feel you can almost hear them.

Photography · Cycles
Artist 03

Ryoji Ikeda

Japanese artist who transforms sound, light, and raw data into stark immersive environments. He uses ultrasonic frequencies and high-contrast digital imagery to push the limits of perception. Technology serves the concept — never the other way around.

Data · Audio Visual
Artist 04

Refik Anadol

Media artist who transforms real-world datasets — sound recordings, weather patterns, brain signals — into living visual installations using AI and machine learning. His work explores memory, time, and the cycle of data.

AI · Generative Art
Artist 05

Stanley Kubrick

2001: A Space Odyssey uses long silences, minimal dialogue, and visually hypnotic sequences to produce psychedelic experience. The film was a primary influence for recreating its sensory effects through audio.

Cinema · Psychedelia
Artist 06

Andy Warhol

Marilyn Diptych — repeated images shifting through colour — creates a trance-like rhythm that echoes psychedelic experience while critiquing mass media and celebrity culture. Repetition as meaning-making.

Pop Art · Repetition
04 — The DNA Idea

Translating
life into music

DNA is perhaps the most fundamental cycle in all of biology — four bases (A, T, C, G) repeating in patterns that encode every living thing.

The core idea of this project: map each DNA base to a musical note, and let the sequence of life itself become a progression of sound.

With the help of AI to translate the genetic code into actual notes, played by virtual instruments and layered into the final installation audio — the project becomes a literal translation of biology into music.

A — T — C — G become notes in an infinite biological score.

05 — Musical References

Compositions that
live in cycles

01

Boléro

Maurice Ravel · 1928

Built almost entirely on one rhythmic snare pattern — a mechanical clock of sound. Repetition is the architecture, the melody, and the drama. An entire piece that is one single cycle, growing.

02

The Four Seasons

Antonio Vivaldi · 1725

Recurring ritornello themes return throughout each concerto, mirroring the seasonal cycles of Earth. Nature encoded as structure in music — the concept of this project in classical form.

03

Symphony No. 9

Ludwig van Beethoven · 1824

Cyclic motifs recurring across all four movements, culminating in the Ode to Joy. The brain recognises the pattern and rewards itself with dopamine — repetition as neurological pleasure.

04

The Dark Side of the Moon

Pink Floyd · 1973

Cycles of recurring elements throughout — including the heartbeat that opens and closes the album. The primary sonic reference for this installation: spatial audio, sensory overload, and the out-of-body experience of listening.

06 — The Installation

Concept: The
Heartbeat Room

Design

A small dark room with a tight-shut door filtering outside sound. At centre: a screen playing the video on an endless loop. Side panels project long rays of light into the room's corners, shaped by fog machines into tangible straight lines you can reach through.

Audio

Begins with a calm heartbeat. Sounds of laughter, nature, and analogue DNA beeps layer in, creating hypnotic, overwhelming presence. Sounds mix and loop, end to end — surprising yet predictable. The audio fades back to the heartbeat and the cycle begins again.

Visual

Altered footage of Earth's events — monochrome water flowing through lakes and waterfalls, transitioning into vibrant colour as human life and greenery appear. The video loops seamlessly, beginning and ending without a visible seam.

Feeling Conveyed

The slow pull into the strings of life itself. Birds and waterfalls heard while images shift. The brain rewards pattern recognition. The heartbeat of the video synchronises with the viewer's — calm yet alive. The world is endless and cyclic.
