Artist Statement

On Sound, Cycles,
and Making

AuthorNgo Tuan Khoi
ProjectCycles of Sound
Year2026
MediumAudio · Visual · Installation
Origins

Why I kept coming back
to the idea of repetition

I have always been drawn to music — not just as a listener but as someone who has spent hours at a piano, repeating the same phrase until the fingers remember it without the mind's instruction. That experience of learning through repetition never left me. It became the seed of this project.

When I started researching the concept of cycles, I realised I wasn't looking for something new. I was trying to articulate something I had always felt: that repetition is not monotony. It is the structure through which the brain finds meaning, reward, and eventually, transcendence.

The question that drove the work was simple: if everything in nature moves in cycles — heartbeats, tides, seasons, DNA — then is everything in nature already music? And if so, what does it sound like?

"Practicing classical music needs a form of repetition to memorise the song, as the brain moulds itself through repetitions."

The Work

Watching DNA
become sound

The video below is the centrepiece of the installation. It visualises the process of translating a DNA sequence — the four repeating bases A, T, C, G — into a progression of musical notes, played by virtual instruments and layered with sounds of heartbeats, rain, jungle, and the analogue beep of digital data.

Film — Dna_3
Dna_3 · Installation film · Ngo Tuan Khoi · 2026

The piece is influenced most directly by Pink Floyd's The Dark Side of the Moon, which I listened to throughout the making of this work. The recurring heartbeat that opens and closes the album is not directly borrowed — but it is implied. The concept of a loop that contains an entire world within it is what I was reaching for.

Process

How the work
was made

The audio was built inside Bandlab, layering royalty-free sounds found online — rain, thunder, jungle ambience, a military helicopter, a classroom — with a clock-ticking loop and a DNA beep sequence generated by AI from an actual base-pair pattern. Each layer was altered: reverb extended, depth shifted, spatial audio widened so the sound surrounds you rather than sits in front of you.

The visual component took longer. Early experiments — blurred photographs of a drum kit and piano with grain added to simulate static — felt too illustrative. The breakthrough came from studying Ryoji Ikeda's work, specifically how the technology disappears into the concept. The screen is never the spectacle. The screen is a window.

I switched to found footage of natural events — water, forests, people — altered in post to create a monochrome-to-colour drift that mirrors the audio's shift from stillness to overwhelm. The video was edited to loop seamlessly: the final frame breathes back into the first without a visible seam.

"I like the sense of order and chaos, and trying to find a way for both to exist together."

Reflection

What I understand
now that I didn't

Before this project, I thought about sound and image as separate languages. I finished it understanding that they are the same language at different frequencies. Hiroshi Sugimoto's photographs of waves feel audible. Ryoji Ikeda's data installations feel like something you can hear before you can see. That collapse of the senses into a single experience is what I was trying to produce.

I also learnt that cycles are not about sameness. Bolero repeats one snare pattern for fifteen minutes, and yet it is never the same twice — because the orchestration around it shifts, the listener changes, the room fills. A heartbeat sounds different when you are afraid than when you are calm, even if the interval is identical.

This is what I want the installation to leave with its audience: not a conclusion, but a feeling. The sense of being inside something that was already moving before you arrived, and will continue after you leave.

Key Concepts
  • Biophony

    The collective sound produced by living organisms in a given habitat — coined by Bernie Krause.

  • Geophony

    Natural, non-biological sounds: rain, wind, thunder, water — the percussion of the Earth itself.

  • Cyclic Motif

    A short musical pattern that repeats and transforms across a composition, giving it structural coherence.

  • Sensory Overload

    The deliberate layering of overlapping stimuli to overwhelm and then immerse the viewer — the psychedelic technique.
